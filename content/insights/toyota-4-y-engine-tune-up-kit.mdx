<h3>Distributor Cap, Rotor, and Points</h3>
<p>First things first, pop the two clips holding the distributor cap on. Before yanking it off, pause for a second and note which terminal the number one spark plug wire connects to. A quick snap with your phone or a dab of paint pen will save you a major headache later.</p>
<p>Lift the cap straight off, then pull the rotor off its shaft. Now you’re looking at the ignition points and the condenser. You&#39;ll see two small screws holding the points assembly and one screw for the condenser. Back them out, disconnect the little wire, and lift the old parts out.</p>
<p>Now for the most critical part of the job: setting the gap on the new points from your kit. You&#39;ll need to rotate the engine by hand until the rubbing block on the points rests on the highest point of the distributor cam lobe. Grab your feeler gauge and set the gap—the sweet spot is typically around <strong>0.018 inches (0.45mm)</strong>. It needs to be a snug fit, where the gauge drags just slightly as you pull it through. If that gap is too wide or too tight, it&#39;ll throw your timing and spark quality completely out of whack.</p>
<p>This simple workflow is the foundation for any job on this engine. Start clean, get the right tools, and always think about safety first.</p>

<h3>Reading and Replacing Spark Plugs</h3>
<p>Next up, pull the old spark plugs. But don&#39;t just chuck them in the bin. The condition of the tips tells a story about what’s going on inside each cylinder.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dry and Sooty:</strong> This is a dead giveaway for a rich fuel mixture or a weak spark.</li>
<li><strong>Oily and Wet:</strong> Not a good sign. This points to bigger problems like worn piston rings or failing valve stem seals.</li>
<li><strong>White and Blistered:</strong> A classic symptom of the engine running too lean or having too much timing advance, which can burn a hole right through a piston.</li>
</ul>
<p>Gap your new plugs to spec (usually around <strong>0.032 inches or 0.8mm</strong>) and thread them in by hand first to make sure you don&#39;t cross-thread them. Finish them off with a torque wrench to about <strong>13 ft-lbs (18 Nm)</strong>. Overtightening is a rookie mistake that can damage the cylinder head and turn a simple tune-up into a massive repair job.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>You&#39;re not just swapping parts; you&#39;re doing a health check. A five-second look at those old plugs can diagnose an issue that a dealer would charge you hundreds to find with their diagnostic computer.</p>
</blockquote>

<h3>Fine-Tuning the Carburetor for a Smooth Idle</h3>
<p>With clean air and fuel finally reaching the engine, it&#39;s time to dial in the carburetor. This isn&#39;t some kind of black art; it&#39;s just a matter of making small, careful adjustments and listening to what the engine is telling you. You&#39;re looking for two screws on the side of the carb: the <strong>idle speed screw</strong> and the <strong>air-fuel mixture screw</strong>.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Idle Speed Screw:</strong> This one’s straightforward. It&#39;s a simple stop that prevents the throttle from closing all the way. With the engine fully warmed up, turn this screw clockwise to increase the idle RPM or counter-clockwise to lower it. You&#39;re shooting for a smooth, steady idle right around <strong>550 RPM</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Air-Fuel Mixture Screw:</strong> This adjustment is a bit more delicate. It fine-tunes the air-to-fuel ratio at idle. First, turn the screw in gently until it <em>barely</em> seats—don&#39;t crank on it. Then, back it out about <strong>1.5 to 2 full turns</strong> as a starting point. Start the engine. From here, make tiny, quarter-turn adjustments. Listen for the engine speed to increase and smooth out. Turning it in too far (lean) will make it stumble; turning it out too far (rich) will make it run rough and smell like gas. Your goal is to find the &quot;sweet spot&quot; where the engine idles fastest and most consistently.</li>
</ul>
<p>After you&#39;ve found the perfect setting on the mixture screw, the idle speed will likely have changed. Go back to the idle speed screw and adjust the RPM back down to your <strong>550 RPM</strong> target. Bouncing between these two adjustments a couple of times will lock in a rock-solid idle, give you a crisp throttle response, and restore the low-end grunt you need to get work done without sputtering.</p>

<h3>Setting the Valve Lash</h3>
<p>Valve lash—that tiny gap between the rocker arm and the valve stem—is absolutely critical. If it&#39;s too tight, the valves can&#39;t fully close, causing a loss of compression and eventually burning out the valve seats. Too loose, and you&#39;ll get that classic, annoying &quot;tick-tick-tick&quot; at idle, which is the sound of your valvetrain slowly hammering itself into oblivion.</p>
<p>First things first, make sure the engine is completely cold. Pop the valve cover off. You&#39;ll need to turn the engine over by hand to get the piston for the cylinder you&#39;re adjusting to Top Dead Center (TDC) on its compression stroke. The easy way to tell you&#39;re there? Both rocker arms for that cylinder will be loose.</p>
<p>Now, it&#39;s time for the feeler gauges. These specs aren&#39;t suggestions; they&#39;re essential.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Intake Valve Clearance (Cold):</strong> 0.008 inches (0.20mm)</li>
<li><strong>Exhaust Valve Clearance (Cold):</strong> 0.012 inches (0.30mm)</li>
</ul>
<p>Gently slide the correct gauge between the rocker and the valve stem. You&#39;re aiming for a light drag, not forcing it in or having it fall through. If the gap is off, loosen the lock nut, turn the adjustment screw until the feel is just right, and then tighten the lock nut back down without letting the screw move. Go through this process for all eight valves, one by one.</p>
<p>With a full tune-up kit, you&#39;re not just replacing parts; you&#39;re restoring the engine&#39;s core functions. Valve clearances get set to <strong>0.008 inches intake (hot) and 0.014 inches exhaust</strong>, spark plugs get torqued down properly, and timing chain checks help prevent the rare <strong>5%</strong> failure rate from excessive wear. We&#39;ve seen these kits cut emissions by a solid <strong>20–25%</strong>, helping keep your fleet compliant with standards like 29 CFR 1910.178.</p>

<h3>Why Does It Still Run Rough After a Full Tune Up?</h3>
<p>This is easily the most frustrating part of the job. You&#39;ve swapped every part, torqued everything down, set the timing, and tweaked the carb, but the engine is still missing or sputtering. This feels just like when a JCB throws a code right after a new sensor is installed. Before you start throwing wrenches, let&#39;s look at the usual suspects. Nine times out of ten, it&#39;s something simple that got overlooked.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vacuum Leaks:</strong> This is culprit #1, hands down. An old, cracked vacuum hose is all it takes to completely throw off your air-fuel mixture. The easiest way to check is to grab a can of carb cleaner and give short, controlled sprays around the base of the carburetor and where any vacuum lines connect. If you hear the engine RPMs change, you&#39;ve found your leak.</li>
<li><strong>Weak Fuel Pump:</strong> All the new filters in the world won&#39;t make up for a fuel pump that&#39;s on its last legs. If the engine fires up okay but then bogs down or dies completely under load, the pump might not be keeping up with the demand.</li>
<li><strong>Incorrect Firing Order:</strong> We&#39;ve all done it. In the rush to get things back together, it&#39;s easy to mix up a wire. Take a second and double-check that your spark plug wires are routed to the distributor cap in the correct <strong>1-3-4-2</strong> sequence.</li>
<li><strong>Worn Distributor Shaft:</strong> If there&#39;s slop in the distributor shaft bushings, your point gap will constantly change. This causes the spark timing to wander all over the place, which is a classic cause of a rough, unstable idle.</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>Don&#39;t be too quick to blame the new parts you just got from Flat Earth Equipment. Always start your troubleshooting with the simplest and cheapest fixes first, like those vacuum lines.</p>

<h3>What Are the Most Common Mistakes to Avoid?</h3>
<p>I see the same handful of simple mistakes trip people up time and time again. Avoiding these will save you a world of headache and make sure your tune-up actually lasts.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Over-tightening Spark Plugs:</strong> Just don&#39;t do it. Cranking down on a spark plug is the fastest way to strip the threads in an aluminum cylinder head. That turns a simple job into a nightmare repair. Use a torque wrench—it&#39;s what they&#39;re made for.</li>
<li><strong>Guessing on Gaps:</strong> &quot;Eyeballing&quot; the spark plug or point gap is a guaranteed ticket to poor performance and frustration. It takes seconds to use a proper feeler gauge. Precision is key here.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring the Wires:</strong> You can have the best distributor and brand-new plugs, but if you&#39;re trying to send that spark through old, cracked, and brittle wires, you&#39;re going to have a weak spark. Give them a good inspection and replace them if they show any signs of deterioration.</li>
</ol>
