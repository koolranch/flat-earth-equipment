**P0087** means the ECU is seeing fuel rail pressure below the commanded target. On JCB common-rail diesels (3CX, Loadall, and similar), that usually shows up as hard starting, stumble under load, or limp mode.

Start with supply-side checks (filters, lift pump, air leaks) before replacing expensive rail or pump parts. The suction control valve (SCV) on the high-pressure pump is a frequent failure point — but it is not the only one.

Related: [JCB fault codes hub](/brand/jcb/fault-codes) · [Service Master codes overview](/insights/jcb-service-master-fault-codes-list-and-manual-reset)

## What P0087 Means on a JCB

The ECU compares **commanded** rail pressure to **actual** rail pressure from the rail sensor. When actual pressure stays too low, it sets P0087 and often derates the engine to protect the high-pressure pump and injectors.

Typical symptoms:

- Cranks but will not start
- Hard cold starts
- Hesitation or power loss under load
- Rough idle that improves at higher RPM
- Active P0087 with derate / limp mode

## Likely Causes (Check in This Order)

| Priority | Cause | Why it triggers P0087 |
|----------|--------|------------------------|
| 1 | Clogged primary / secondary fuel filter | Starves the high-pressure pump |
| 2 | Weak lift (transfer) pump or air leak on suction side | Low supply pressure / aeration |
| 3 | Failed or sticking SCV (suction control valve) | Cannot meter fuel into the HP pump |
| 4 | Faulty rail pressure sensor or wiring | False low reading |
| 5 | Failing high-pressure fuel pump | Cannot build rail pressure (often after metal contamination) |

The [JCB fault hub](/brand/jcb/fault-codes) lists P0087 with the same first checks: filter, lift-pump prime, then suction-side leaks.

## Field Diagnosis Steps

### 1. Confirm the code and live data

With a scanner that can read live data (Service Master or a capable OBD tool):

- Note **commanded** vs **actual** rail pressure at key-on and at idle
- A large gap (command high, actual low) points to delivery failure, not just a stored ghost code
- Clear the code only after you have a plan — if it returns immediately under load, the fault is active

### 2. Fuel filters first

Replace both primary and secondary fuel filters if service history is unknown or overdue. Contaminated diesel is the most common root cause behind sticky SCVs and weak supply pressure.

### 3. Lift pump and suction side

Measure low-side supply pressure at the filter housing / test port if equipped. Many JCB setups expect roughly **5–10 PSI** at crank or idle from a healthy lift pump. Near-zero or bouncing pressure suggests a weak pump, collapsed hose, or restricted tank pickup.

Also check:

- Soft or collapsed flexible suction lines
- Loose clamps / cracked fittings (air ingress)
- Tank pickup screen clogged with sludge

Related part: [JCB 320/A7161 fuel lift pump](/parts/320A7161)

### 4. SCV electrical and mechanical checks

The SCV meters low-pressure fuel into the high-pressure pump. Failures are often electrical (open/shorted coil) or mechanical (sticking plunger from dirt or wax).

**Resistance check (key OFF):**

1. Unplug the SCV connector
2. Measure coil resistance across the valve pins
3. Many JCB SCVs read about **2–3 Ω** when healthy — open circuit (OL) or a large out-of-range reading usually means replace the valve
4. Also check harness continuity and connector corrosion; a good valve will not fix a broken wire

**Mechanical clue:** If resistance is OK but the engine stumbles, a light tap on the SCV body (plastic handle — do not damage the valve) that briefly smooths running can indicate a sticking plunger. Treat that as a replace-not-clean decision for a lasting repair.

### 5. Only then consider the high-pressure pump

If filters, lift pump, air leaks, sensor/wiring, and SCV all check out, inspect for metal in the fuel filter. Fine glitter-like particles often mean the high-pressure pump is shedding metal — at that point you are looking at pump, injectors, rail, and a full system flush, not an SCV-only job.

## SCV Replacement Notes

Keep the high-pressure pump opening clean. Dirt in a common-rail system is expensive.

1. Clean the pump and SCV area before opening anything
2. Relieve residual fuel pressure carefully (rag over the fitting)
3. Unplug the harness, remove mounting bolts, pull the old SCV straight out
4. Wipe the bore, install the new valve with lubricated O-rings immediately
5. Torque fasteners to the service-manual spec for your model
6. Replace fuel filters in the same visit
7. Prime with the hand primer until firm, then start and check for leaks

Cleaning an old SCV is a limp-home stopgap at best. Coil and seal wear are not fixed by brake cleaner.

## If P0087 Returns After a New SCV

Work backward through supply again:

1. Verify both filters were actually replaced and primed
2. Re-check lift-pump pressure under load, not only at idle
3. Inspect tank pickup and suction hoses
4. Compare commanded vs actual rail pressure — if actual is healthy but the code persists, suspect the rail pressure sensor / wiring
5. Cut open the new filter if metal appears; stop and plan a full HPFP contamination repair

## FAQ

### Can I drive / work with an active P0087?

Not recommended. Derate protects the pump and injectors; continuing under load with low rail pressure risks a much larger repair.

### Does a bad SCV cause regen problems?

Indirectly. Poor rail pressure can worsen combustion and soot loading, which makes DPF / regen issues more frequent. Fix fuel delivery first, then address emissions codes.
