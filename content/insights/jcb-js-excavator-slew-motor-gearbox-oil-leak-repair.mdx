<h2>Diagnosing That Puddle Under Your Slew Motor</h2>
<p>You see that growing stain every morning. The service writer at the dealer might call it a quick seal replacement, but anyone who&#39;s turned wrenches in the dirt knows that&#39;s just the start. That drip means downtime, an angry foreman, and the gnawing feeling your slew gearbox is about to eat itself.</p>
<p>Let’s cut the fluff. Here&#39;s why your JCB is bleeding gear oil.</p>
<p>This isn&#39;t a seal problem. The seal is just the victim. The real culprit is almost always the output shaft bearing buried deep inside the slew gearbox. After thousands of hours swinging heavy buckets, that bearing develops just a tiny bit of play. It’s not much, but it’s enough to let the main output shaft wobble—and that wobble absolutely destroys the delicate lip of the output seal.</p>
<p>Just slapping a new seal in there without touching the bearing is a guaranteed comeback job. You&#39;ll button it all up, feel good about it for a minute, and then two months later, that same dark puddle will be back to greet you. You have to fix the root cause, not just the symptom.</p>
<h3>Pinpointing The Real Failure</h3>
<p>First thing&#39;s first: confirm exactly where the leak is coming from. Are you dealing with thick, stinky gear oil from the slew gearbox, or is it the thinner hydraulic fluid from the slew motor itself?</p>
<p>Grab a can of degreaser and clean the entire area around the motor and gearbox until it&#39;s spotless. Then, run the machine for a few minutes, slewing the house back and forth. This simple test tells you everything.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Leak from the gearbox weep hole:</strong> This points directly to a failed gearbox output seal and, by extension, the shot bearing behind it.</li>
<li><strong>Leak between the motor and gearbox:</strong> This is likely the motor shaft seal. That means hydraulic fluid is leaking down into the gearbox.</li>
<li><strong>Leak from a hydraulic line:</strong> Don&#39;t overlook the simple stuff. Check all your fittings—sometimes the easiest fix is the right one.</li>
</ul>
<p>The slew gearbox oil leak is one of the most common headaches on these machines. The failure almost always happens right at the output seal. In most cases, the breakdown starts with bearing misalignment, which creates a perfect path for oil to escape. For popular JCB JS series excavators like the <strong>JS220</strong> or <strong>JS260</strong>, that gearbox holds <strong>5 liters</strong> of a specific <strong>JCB HD90 Gear Oil (API-GL-5)</strong>. You can learn more about the common failure points of excavator swing motors from other industry experts.</p>

<p>You’re not just a parts changer. A real tech understands the chain of failure. The worn bearing creates shaft play. The shaft play kills the seal. The dead seal causes the leak. Fix the bearing, and you fix the leak for good.</p>
</blockquote>

<h2>Pulling The Slew Motor And Gearbox From The Machine</h2>
<p>Alright, time to get your hands dirty. We&#39;re done talking; now comes the heavy lifting. Pulling the slew motor and gearbox assembly isn&#39;t just about brute force—it&#39;s about doing it smart so you don&#39;t break something expensive or, worse, get yourself hurt.</p>

<p>First, swing the house so the counterweight is parked directly over one of the track idlers. <h3>Disconnecting And Lifting The Assembly</h3>
<p>With the machine stable and clean, the next step is managing the hydraulics. Get a big drain pan under there because you&#39;re going to make a mess. Before you disconnect a single line, use a paint marker or colored zip ties to label every hose and the port it came from. Don&#39;t trust your memory. Swapping a case drain line with a pressure line during reassembly will blow seals and could even crack the motor housing.</p>

<p><strong>Pro Tip:</strong> As soon as you pull a line, immediately cap the hose and plug the port on the motor. Use proper hydraulic caps, not rags stuffed in the holes. A little heat from a torch can help persuade the really stubborn ones, but be extremely careful around any residual hydraulic fluid.</p>
<p>Rig your lifting chains securely around the motor and gearbox assembly. Use a shop crane or the excavator&#39;s own bucket with a certified sling to take the weight. Slowly lift the unit straight up and out. It&#39;s heavy—we&#39;re talking <strong>several hundred pounds</strong>—so keep your hands and feet clear at all times.</p>
<h3>Splitting The Motor From The Gearbox</h3>
<p>With the whole unit on your workbench or a sturdy pallet, you can now separate the hydraulic slew motor from the planetary gearbox. This is a crucial step for a proper inspection. You&#39;ll see a ring of bolts holding the two halves together.</p>
<p>Remove these bolts carefully, and the motor should slide right out of the gearbox housing. Be ready for some residual oil to spill out. Once they&#39;re separated, you&#39;ll have a clear view of the splined motor shaft and the input side of the gearbox.</p>
<p>This is your chance to hunt for damage beyond the main output seal leak. Check the motor shaft seal for any tell-tale signs of leaking hydraulic fluid. Inspect the splines on both the motor shaft and inside the gearbox for any wear, chipping, or twisting. Finding this kind of damage <em>now</em> saves you from bolting a failing motor back onto your freshly rebuilt gearbox. <h3>Tearing Down The Planetary Gearbox</h3>
<p>Start by carefully disassembling the planetary gear set. Lay out every single component in the exact order you removed it. This is a perfect time to use your phone—snap a picture at each stage. <h3>Pressing Out The Failed Bearing And Seal</h3>
<p>Once the gearbox is stripped down, you can finally get to the culprits: the main output shaft bearing and the seal. This is a job for your heavy-duty press. Don&#39;t even think about using a hammer here unless you want to risk cracking the housing.</p>
<p>Get the gearbox housing supported securely on the press bed. You&#39;ll need the right size driver or a large socket that only contacts the bearing&#39;s outer race. Apply slow, steady, even pressure to push the old bearing and seal out. They can be stubborn, but brute force is not your friend.</p>
<p>With the old parts out, it’s time for the most critical inspection of the whole job. Check the output shaft itself, paying close attention to the machined surfaces where the bearing and seal ride. Look for scoring, grooves, or a polished &quot;wear ring&quot; from the old seal lip. A slight groove is all it takes to chew up a new seal in a few weeks. If you find anything more than light polishing, that shaft needs repair or replacement. Don&#39;t cut corners here.</p>
<h3>Meticulous Cleaning And Reassembly</h3>
<p>This is where the pros really shine. A quick wipe with a rag isn&#39;t cleaning. Every gear, every bearing race, and every nook and cranny inside that housing has to be spotless. Use a parts washer or a solvent tank, followed by compressed air, to get every last bit of grime and metal flake out of there.</p>
<p>When you install the new bearing, <strong>never press on the inner race</strong>. Use your press and a correctly sized driver to apply pressure <em>only</em> to the outer race. Make sure it goes in perfectly straight and seats all the way down in its bore. For the new seal, a light coat of grease on the outer diameter and the inner lip makes installation smoother. Gently tap it into place with a seal driver until it’s perfectly square and flush with the housing.</p>

<h2>Reassembly, Installation, And Final Testing</h2>
<p>You’ve done the hard part. The gearbox is clean, the new parts are in, and everything is buttoned up on the bench. Now it’s time to get this unit back in the machine where it belongs and prove the fix is solid. Don&#39;t get sloppy on this last leg of the job—a rushed reassembly can undo all your careful work.</p>
<p>First up, let’s marry the hydraulic slew motor back to the gearbox. A light coat of grease on the motor shaft splines will help things slide together smoothly. Carefully align and guide the motor into the gearbox housing. Get all the bolts started by hand in a star pattern to make sure the motor seats evenly, <em>then</em> break out the torque wrench and bring them to spec. This little step is crucial for preventing a warped mounting flange, which could create a brand new leak.</p>
<h3>Getting The Assembly Back In The Machine</h3>
<p>With the motor and gearbox reconnected as a single unit, it&#39;s time for the big lift. Rig your chains securely and use your shop crane to carefully lower the whole assembly back onto the slew ring. This is pretty much a blind drop, so it&#39;s a two-person job. Have a spotter guide you in and keep your hands clear.</p>
<p>Once it&#39;s seated, start all the main mounting bolts by hand. These are high-grade bolts, and you absolutely need to use a thread-locking compound on them. Torque them down in a crisscross pattern to the manufacturer&#39;s spec. Whatever you do, don&#39;t just hammer them with an impact wrench until they stop turning. Uneven torque can put the entire gearbox in a bind and lead to catastrophic bearing failure down the road.</p>

<p>With the hoses connected, you can fill the gearbox itself. Don’t just grab any old gear oil. A JS220, for example, will take about <strong>5 liters</strong>. Pour it in slowly until the oil level reaches the bottom of the fill plug opening.</p>

<h3>The Final Shakedown Test</h3>
<p>Before you hand the keys back, you need to run a full system test. This is more than just a quick check for leaks; it&#39;s about verifying that the <strong>JCB JS excavator slew motor gearbox oil leak repair</strong> was a complete success.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Bleed the Hydraulic Circuit:</strong> Fire up the machine at a low idle. It&#39;s almost guaranteed that some air got into the slew motor circuit. Gently slew the house left and right, just a few feet each way, to work that trapped air back to the tank. <li><strong>Smoothness and Sound Check:</strong> With the air bled out, run the house through its full range of motion several times. Listen like a hawk for any grinding, popping, or whining from the gearbox. The swing should feel smooth and consistent, with no jerking or hesitation.</li>
<li><strong>Slew Brake Engagement:</strong> On level ground, get the house swinging and then let go of the controls. The slew brake should engage smoothly and bring the turret to a firm, clean stop without any harsh clunking.</li>
<li><strong>Load Test and Final Leak Check:</strong> Now for the real test. Pick up a full bucket of dirt or rock to put the system under a proper working load. Slew back and forth several times. <h3>What Kind Of Oil Does My JCB JS Slew Gearbox Need?</h3>
<p>Don&#39;t just walk into the auto parts store and grab the first bottle of 90-weight you see. The JCB JS series planetary gearboxes are picky. You need a high-quality <strong>API GL-5 gear oil</strong>, specifically something like JCB HD90 or an equivalent that meets the MIL-L-2105 spec.</p>
<p>Using the wrong stuff will cause premature wear because it lacks the right extreme pressure (EP) additives needed to protect those gears under thousands of pounds of force. A JS220 takes around <strong>5 liters</strong>, so make sure you have enough on hand before you start. We can get you the right spec oil along with your seals and bearings, no dealer required.</p>
<h3>Why Is My Slew Motor Weak After The Repair?</h3>
<p>If the slew is weak but you don&#39;t have any leaks, the problem is almost certainly on the hydraulic side, not in the gearbox you just rebuilt. There are a few common culprits to chase down.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Trapped Air:</strong> This is the most likely issue. You need to bleed the air out of the hydraulic motor circuit by slowly swinging the house back and forth.</li>
<li><strong>Contamination:</strong> A tiny piece of grit or a shred from a shop rag that got into an open line during reassembly can jam up a relief valve or mess with the motor&#39;s internals.</li>
<li><strong>Crossed Lines:</strong> It happens. Double-check that you didn&#39;t accidentally swap the pressure lines. It&#39;s an easy mistake to make when you&#39;re tired and covered in grease.</li>
</ul>
