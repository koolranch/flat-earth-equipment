<h3>Decoding the Hydraulic Schematic</h3>
<p>The hydraulic diagram looks complex, but it’s just a map of the machine’s circulatory system. Every line represents a hose or tube, and every symbol is a component doing a specific job. To start, find the hydraulic pump—it’s the heart of the system—and trace the lines coming from it.</p>
<p>You&#39;ll see lines leading to the main control valve, which directs fluid to different functions like lift and tilt. Look for key symbols along the way:</p>
<ul>

<li><strong>Cylinders:</strong> Represented by a simple rectangle with a T-shaped line inside, showing the piston and rod that do the actual work.</li>

<h3>Essential Service Procedures</h3>
<p>Beyond just hitting the right torque values, following the correct procedure is what separates a professional repair from a botched job. Here are two procedures where the steps you take matter just as much as the final twist of the wrench.</p>
<p><strong>Bleeding the Brake System</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>Top off the master cylinder with fresh DOT 3 brake fluid.</li>
<li>Start at the wheel cylinder farthest from the master cylinder to work the air out of the longest line first.</li>
<li>Attach a clear hose to the bleeder screw and submerge the other end in a container of fresh brake fluid.</li>
<li>Have a helper pump the brake pedal several times, then hold it down firmly.</li>
<li>Crack open the bleeder screw until the fluid flows, then tighten it <em>before</em> your helper releases the pedal.</li>
<li>Repeat this process until you see no more air bubbles in the hose. Then move to the next wheel.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Adjusting Mast Chains</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>Raise the forks about one foot off the ground to put some tension on the chains.</li>
<li>Feel the tension of both lift chains; they should feel equal. A loose chain will cause jerky lifting.</li>
<li>If they&#39;re uneven, find the adjusting nuts on the chain anchors.</li>
<li>Loosen the lock nut, then turn the adjusting nut until both chains have the same amount of give.</li>
<li>You&#39;re looking for roughly <strong>1/2 inch of slack</strong> when you press on them.</li>
<li>Once they&#39;re even, tighten the lock nuts securely to hold the adjustment.</li>
</ol>
