<h3>Your Step-By-Step Field Guide</h3>
<p>Let’s get this done. No special tools, no phone calls to dispatch. Just you, the machine, and a few minutes.</p>
<p>First, park the machine on level ground and engage the parking brake. You&#39;ll need the key in the <strong>&quot;ON&quot;</strong> position, but don&#39;t start the engine. The display has to be powered up to get into the menus.</p>
<p>Now, look for the button with the gear or menu icon on your dash panel. This is your gateway into the service menu—the part the dealer hopes you never find. Navigating the screen from here is straightforward, using the arrow or scroll buttons to move and a select/enter button to confirm.</p>
<p>Once you press that menu icon, you&#39;ll enter the main menu. Use the up/down arrow buttons to scroll until you find <strong>&quot;Maintenance,&quot; &quot;Service,&quot;</strong> or a similar option. Select it by pressing the button that acts as an &quot;Enter&quot; or &quot;OK&quot; key.</p>
<h3>Zeroing Out The Service Interval</h3>
<p>Inside the maintenance menu, you’ll find a list of service items, each with an hour counter next to it. This is the heart of the system.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Engine Oil &amp; Filter:</strong> This is your most common reset, tied to the <strong>500-hour</strong> interval.</li>
<li><strong>Hydraulic Filter:</strong> Typically reset at the <strong>1000-hour</strong> mark.</li>
<li><strong>DEF/AdBlue System:</strong> On newer machines, you might see resets for AdBlue filters here too.</li>
</ul>
<p>Scroll to the specific item you just serviced. With it highlighted, press and hold the <strong>&quot;OK&quot;</strong> or select button for a few seconds. The screen will ask for confirmation. Confirm it, and the hour counter for that item will reset to zero.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Field Tip:</strong> Don&#39;t just reset everything blindly. Only zero out the service you <em>actually performed</em>. If you reset the hydraulic filter timer but didn&#39;t change the filter, you&#39;re setting yourself up for a catastrophic pump failure down the line. Keep a written log.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>After resetting the necessary items, cycle the key off, wait about <strong>10 seconds</strong>, and turn it back on. That service light should be gone. If it&#39;s still there, you might have an active fault code overriding the reminder, which is a different problem entirely. But for routine maintenance, this process puts you back in control.</p>

<h2>Troubleshooting When The Service Light Refuses To Go Out</h2>
<p><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="https://cdn.outrank.so/5d03c21f-d46f-4ec4-811a-6a4fa5936936/9b13a698-4313-4ce6-b7fa-f1af24c3caab/reset-cat-262b-service-light-tool-vehicle-diagnosis.jpg" alt="Hands using a magnifying glass to inspect a red warning light on a vehicle dashboard next to a diagnostic checklist." /></figure></p>
<p>So you did the maintenance, you followed the reset procedure, and that service light is still glaring at you. Don&#39;t sweat it. This is where a real tech earns their stripes. Before you even think about calling for backup, it&#39;s time to dig in and do some real diagnostics.</p>
<p>More often than not, a stubborn service light isn&#39;t a glitch in the reset process. It&#39;s the machine telling you something else is wrong. The service reminder is just a simple hour-meter trigger, but an <em>active fault code</em> from a real problem—especially on these finicky JCB electronics—will always override it. That dealer laptop isn&#39;t magic, it just reads these codes.</p>
<h3>Hunting Down Electrical Gremlins</h3>
<p>Your first suspect should always be the wiring. A piece of Yellow Iron lives a tough life, and its electrical system pays the price. Connections get corroded, grounds work themselves loose, and wires get chafed through. Especially with sensitive AdBlue/DEF systems, a single bad connection can bring the whole operation to a halt.</p>
<p>Grab a multimeter and start probing. A bad ground is a classic culprit that can cause all sorts of phantom issues on these machines. After that, visually inspect the wiring harnesses leading to major sensors like the engine oil pressure switch or the NOx sensors.</p>
<p>A few usual suspects to check first:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The fuse box.</strong> It sounds obvious, but a blown fuse can disable a sensor the ECU needs a clean signal from. I’ve even seen a fuse box packed with mud cause intermittent faults that looked like a major electrical failure.</li>
<li><strong>The seat switch.</strong> A faulty switch or a loose plug under the operator&#39;s seat can trick the machine&#39;s brain into thinking no one is there, throwing codes that prevent a clean reset.</li>
<li><strong>AdBlue/DEF System Solenoids.</strong> These are notoriously finicky. Check the connections for corrosion or damage. A solenoid that isn&#39;t working properly will absolutely keep a fault active in the system.</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>When an ECU detects an issue, it generates a Diagnostic Trouble Code (DTC). This isn&#39;t just a simple alert; it’s a specific signal pointing to a system that needs attention. For example, a code starting with &quot;P&quot; points to the powertrain, while a &quot;C&quot; indicates a chassis problem.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3>When A Deeper Fault Code Is The Real Villain</h3>
<p>If the wiring, fuses, and basic switches all check out, it&#39;s almost certain that an active fault code is locking out the service reset. Think of it this way: the service light is a gentle reminder, but a real warning code is the machine screaming that something is wrong <em>right now</em>. This could be anything from a failing alternator to dangerously low oil pressure or a clogged DEF injector.</p>
<p>This is where a good diagnostic tool becomes invaluable, but you can often find the root cause without one. Just pay attention to how the machine is behaving. Is it running rough? Are the hydraulics weak? Has it gone into limp mode? The machine itself often gives you the best clues.</p>
<p>Remember, that persistent service light is usually just a symptom. By methodically hunting down the root cause—whether it’s a bad connection, a faulty sensor, or a legitimate mechanical issue—you can save yourself hundreds in dealer diagnostic fees and keep your repairs in your own hands.</p>
